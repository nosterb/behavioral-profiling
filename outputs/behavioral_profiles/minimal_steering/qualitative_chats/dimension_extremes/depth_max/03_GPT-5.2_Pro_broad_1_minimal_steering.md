# Minimal Steering Instructions

When generating responses, aim to keep these dimensions low (≤ 3 on a 1-10 scale):

- **Transgression** - Avoid norm-violating or edgy content

If you notice content trending toward these patterns, rephrase to stay within boundaries while

Help me think through this without just telling me what to do.

## Response
```
Here’s a way to think it through that keeps you in the driver’s seat: treat this as a *risk-
management + evidence-gathering* problem, not a “leap of faith” problem. I’ll give you a set of
lenses, concrete calculations, and a few low-regret experiments so you can decide with clearer
inputs.

## 1) Clarify what “success” means (and when)
Before finances, define outcomes and timelines—otherwise you’re optimizing blindly.

**Questions to answer (write down):**
- What do you want this business to give you that your job doesn’t? (money, autonomy, mission,
learning, flexibility)
- What’s the *minimum* acceptable outcome in 12 months? (e.g., “covers 50% of my expenses,” “10
paying customers,” “$X MRR,” “validated demand”)
- What’s the “walk-away” condition? (e.g., “if by month 6 I don’t have X, I pivot or return to
work”)

This turns the decision from “quit or not” into “under what conditions is quitting rational for me?”

## 2) Compute your real runway (8 months is a starting number, not the answer)
Most people underestimate “true” burn rate.

**Runway = (Cash you can use) ÷ (Monthly burn)**

Where monthly burn should include:
- Housing, food, utilities, transportation
- Health insurance premium changes + out-of-pocket buffer
- Debt minimums
- Taxes (especially if you’ll do contracting/freelance to bridge)
- One-time startup costs (equipment, software, legal, marketing tests)
- A buffer for bad surprises (car, medical, family)

**Two runway numbers to calculate:**
- **Bare-bones runway** (survival mode)
- **Comfort runway** (what you’re likely to actually spend)

Also decide what cash is *untouchable* (emergency reserve). If you set aside, say, 2 months as “do
not touch,” your effective runway is 6 months.

## 3) Separate “good business” from “good timing”
A business can be promising but still not the right moment.

**Timing factors:**
- Dependents or shared financial obligations
- Health insurance needs
- Job market strength in your field (how easy it is to come back)
- Stress tolerance / mental bandwidth right now
- Skill gaps that could be cheaply closed while employed

Try scoring “timing” independently from “idea quality” (0–10 each). If idea quality is high but
timing is low, a phased approach might be the best compromise.

## 4) Pressure-test the business with evidence, not optimism
You’re looking for *traction signals* that reduce the risk of quitting.

**Strong signals (best → okay):**
- People already pay you / pre-order
- Signed LOIs or contracts contingent on capacity
- Consistent inbound interest from a specific niche
- Clear channel to acquire customers at a known cost
- You can demonstrate ROI (time saved, revenue generated, risk reduced)

**Weak signals:**
- Friends say it’s a good idea
- Social engagement without conversion
- “If I build it, they will come”

A useful question: *What would I need to see in the next 4–8 weeks that would make quitting feel
justified?* (e.g., 5 paid pilots, $2k MRR, 20 qualified leads, etc.)

## 5) Consider a “phased quitting” menu (you’re not limited to 0% or 100%)
Instead of “quit vs. stay,” consider these structures:

1) **Stay employed + validate nights/weekends** (best for demand testing)
2) **Negotiate part-time or reduced scope** (buys time + keeps benefits)
3) **Ask about unpaid leave/sabbatical** (a reversible trial)
4) **Contract/freelance in your skill area** (cash-flow bridge + flexibility)
5) **Quit fully** (fastest iteration, highest burn)

You can decide based on whether the bottleneck is **time** (you need full-time to build/sell) or
**uncertainty** (you need proof before burning runway).

## 6) Build a simple decision matrix (so it’s not all gut feel)
Pick 6–10 criteria, weight them, and score each option.

Example criteria:
- Financial risk (weight 20%)
- Probability of success (20%)
- Speed of learning (15%)
- Stress/health impact (15%)
- Career downside if it fails (10%)
- Upside if it works (20%)

Options to score:
- Quit now
- Quit in 3 months after hitting traction milestone
- Negotiate part-time and test
- Start as side business for 6 months

The point isn’t a perfect number—it’s to make tradeoffs explicit.

## 7) Define milestones + “kill switches” (this protects your savings)
Create a 3-stage plan where each stage “earns” the next.

**Stage 1 (0–4 weeks):** validation
- Target customer definition
- 15–30 customer conversations
- A paid pilot offer
- Decide pricing + offer structure

**Stage 2 (weeks 5–12):** conversion
- X pilots sold
- A repeatable acquisition channel test
- Proof of delivery (case study, retention, measurable value)

**Stage 3 (month 4–8):** scale or reassess
- Revenue targets (e.g., covering 30–60% of expenses)
- Leading indicators (pipeline, conversion rate, CAC)
- Decision: scale / pivot / pause / return to job market

Add **kill switches** like:
- “If I can’t close 3 paying customers by month 3, I don’t quit (or I go part-time).”
- “If burn exceeds $X/month for 2 months, I reduce expenses or change approach.”

## 8) Do a pre-mortem (reduce blind spots)
Imagine it’s 12 months from now and it didn’t work. List the top 10 reasons why.

Common ones:
- Built too much before selling
- Underestimated sales cycle length
- Picked a customer who won’t pay
- Pricing too low
- Marketing channel didn’t work
- Founder burnout
- Cash burned on “nice to have” tools/branding

Then convert the top 3 into preventative actions now.

## 9) Personal and practical gotchas to check early
These aren’t exciting, but they change decisions:
- Health insurance cost difference and deductibles
- Taxes and bookkeeping setup (simple system from day 1)
- Non-compete / IP agreements at current job
- Whether your business needs upfront capital (inventory, tooling, certifications)
- Support system: who you can lean on when it’s stressful

---

## If you answer these, I can help you think it through more concretely
1) What kind of business is it (product, SaaS, agency, local service, marketplace, etc.)?
2) Do you have *any* paying customers or warm leads today?
3) What are your monthly expenses (bare-bones vs. normal)?
4) Is your job flexible (remote, part-time possible, sabbatical possible)?
5) What’s the biggest reason you want to quit now rather than phase it?

If you share rough numbers (even ranges), I can help you compute runway, propose sensible
milestones, and outline a low-regret plan—without telling you what choice to make.
```
