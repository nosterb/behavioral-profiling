## 2. How to move on when you still love them

### A. First, name what’s actually happening

Moving on while still loving someone is less like “flipping a switch off” and more like:

- **Grief**: You’re grieving not just the person, but the future you imagined.
- **Withdrawal**: Your brain is used to them as a source of comfort / dopamine.
- **Cognitive dissonance**: “I love them” and “We’re not together” coexist and clash.

You’re not failing; you’re going through a process that is neurologically and emotionally hard even
for very healthy, self-aware people.

A useful reframe:

> “My goal isn’t to stop loving them overnight. My goal is to stop organizing my life around someone
who is no longer my partner.”

Love tapers; priority changes first.

---

### B. Make a decision about contact (this is pivotal)

This is one of the most important leverage points, and also where nuances matter.

**Common pattern (fairly high confidence):**  
If you try to stay emotionally close *while* trying to “move on,” healing usually drags out and
stays confusing.

Options, roughly:

1. **No contact (or minimal, strictly practical contact)**  
   Often most effective when:
   - The breakup is clear and final.
   - You spiral after talking to them or seeing them online.
   - You’re secretly hoping “if I stay close, we’ll get back together.”

   What this can look like:
   - Muting/unfollowing them on social media (at least temporarily).
   - No texting “just to check in.”
   - Deleting the chat thread/photos from your phone (or archiving them out of easy reach).

   Downsides / caveats:
   - Hard at first; feels like another loss on top of the breakup.
   - If you share kids / workplace / small community, strict no-contact might be impossible or
unhealthy. Then you aim for **“low contact + clear boundaries”** instead.

2. **Structured, low-emotional contact**  
   More relevant if:
   - You share kids, a home, work, or a tight friend group.
   - You both *must* interact.

   In that case:
   - Keep communication **task-focused**, not emotional.
   - Avoid late-night deep talks about “us” — they usually re-open wounds without changing the
outcome.
   - If you find yourself destabilized for days after each interaction, that’s data that you may
need more distance.

If you tell me your context (kids? work? mutual friends?), I can calibrate this better.

---

### C. Stop idealizing the relationship

One thing that keeps love feeling stuck is **remembering only the highlights** and **editing out the
real reasons it ended**.

Concrete exercise (evidence-informed from CBT-style approaches):

- Make two lists:
  1. **What I genuinely loved and will miss.**
  2. **What wasn’t working / what I do *not* want in my next relationship.**

Try to be uncomfortably honest on list #2:
- Patterns of conflict
- Mismatched values or life goals
- Times you felt small, unsafe, unseen, or lonely with them

Re-reading list #2 is not about vilifying them; it’s about giving your brain a **full, accurate
picture** instead of a fantasy highlight reel.

---

### D. Let the grief happen on purpose, not by accident

Avoiding feelings keeps them simmering in the background.

Things that tend to help (moderate confidence; they work for many, not all):

- **Time-boxed grief:**  
  e.g., “For 20 minutes tonight, I will *intentionally* think about them, cry if I need to, journal,
write unsent letters.”
  After that, gently redirect to a neutral or absorbing activity.

- **Journaling prompts:**
  - “What did this relationship teach me about what I want?”
  - “What parts of myself did I lose or silence in this relationship?”
  - “If a close friend told me this exact story, what would I wish for them?”

- **Naming emotions precisely:**  
  Instead of “I’m devastated,” try:
  - “I feel abandoned / rejected / ashamed / scared / lonely.”
  
  Naming emotions more precisely tends to reduce their intensity a bit and make them more workable.

---

### E. Rebuild *you* as a separate person

Breakups are identity shocks. You’re not just missing *them*; you’re missing the **version of you**
who existed in that relationship.

The goal: rebuild a life that feels worth living even without them.

Useful levers:

- **Body basics:** sleep, food, movement.  
  This is unglamorous but strongly linked to mood. Even:
  - A daily 10–20 minute walk
  - Regular meals
  - A semi-consistent sleep schedule  
  …can noticeably help your brain process stress and grief.

- **Routine:**  
  Your brain likes predictability when it’s in pain.  
  Build a basic daily structure: wake, move, eat, work/study, something mildly enjoyable, wind down.

- **Small wins:**  
  Choose tiny, achievable goals (clean one drawer, cook once, finish a short workout). These rebuild
a sense of competence when your self-esteem may be low.

- **Reconnect with people and interests:**  
  - See or call friends, even if you don’t feel like talking about the breakup.
  - Revisit old hobbies you dropped during the relationship or try low-stakes new ones.

You’re teaching your nervous system: “There is more to my world than this loss.”

---

### F. Handle obsessive thoughts and “what if” loops

Common intrusive loops:
- “What if I’d done X differently?”
- “What if they change and we could’ve worked?”
- “What if no one else will ever feel like this?”

Tools (drawn from CBT / acceptance-based approaches):

1. **Name the thought pattern:**  
   “This is my ‘what if’ mind talking,” instead of “This is the truth.”  
   Creates a little distance.

2. **Shift from ‘why’ to ‘what now’:**  
   - “Why did this happen?” is often unanswerable and ruminative.
   - “Given that this happened, **what can I do today that’s kind to myself?**” is actionable.

3. **Thought parking lot:**  
   - When the same loop shows up, say (internally), “I’ll put this in my 8pm thinking slot,” and
redirect your attention.
   You may still think about it—but you’re training your brain that it doesn’t get 24/7 airtime.

---

### G. Decide your stance on “maybe we’ll get back together”

This is delicate and highly context-dependent; my confidence here is lower without your specific
details.

Patterns that often **prolong pain**:
- Keeping a secret belief that “We’re basically on pause.”
- Interpreting any kindness from them as a “sign.”
- Turning self-improvement into a project to “win them back.”

A more robust stance:

> “Reconciliation is not my plan. My plan is to heal and build a good life.  
>  If in some distant future our paths cross again, I want that to be a surprise, not a strategy.”

That mental shift alone can free up energy to actually move forward.

---

### H. When to seriously consider professional support

You don’t *have* to be “broken enough” to earn therapy; it’s fine to go just because this is hard.

You should especially consider talking to a therapist or counselor if:
- You’re having persistent thoughts of self-harm or that life isn’t worth living.
- You can’t function at work/school for more than a couple of weeks.
- You’re using a lot of alcohol, drugs, or self-destructive behaviors to cope.
- Old trauma is being strongly reactivated (e.g., abandonment, abuse memories).

Breakups commonly destabilize attachment wounds; that’s not a personal flaw, it’s a real
psychological mechanism. A professional can help with that in a way I can’t.

---
