The **Mona Lisa**, painted by **Leonardo da Vinci**, is one of the most iconic artworks in history,
renowned for its technical mastery, enigmatic subject, and intriguing history. Here's a structured
overview of its story:

### **Creation & Technique**
- **Artist**: Leonardo da Vinci (1452–1519), a Renaissance polymath, began the painting around
**1503** in Florence and worked on it intermittently until his death in 1519. He carried it with him
throughout his life, refining it continuously.
- **Technique**: Leonardo employed **sfumato**, a method using layered glazes to create soft, smoky
transitions between colors and tones. This technique gives the portrait its lifelike depth and the
subject’s elusive smile.
- **Composition**: The figure is framed by a dreamlike, imaginary landscape with winding rivers and
valleys, possibly symbolizing life’s journey. The pyramidal structure and atmospheric perspective
were innovative for the time.

### **Subject Identity**
- **Lisa Gherardini**: Widely believed to depict **Lisa del Giocondo**, wife of Florentine merchant
Francesco del Giocondo. "Mona Lisa" derives from *Monna Lisa* (Italian for "My Lady Lisa").
- **Debates**: Some theories suggest alternative models, including Leonardo’s mother or a self-
portrait, but scholarly consensus favors Lisa Gherardini.

### **Historical Journey**
- **Royal Acquisition**: Leonardo took the painting to France, where it was purchased by **King
Francis I** around 1518. It entered the French royal collection, later housed in the Louvre after
the French Revolution.
- **Napoleon’s Interlude**: Briefly displayed in Napoleon Bonaparte’s private quarters in the early
19th century before returning to the Louvre.

### **Theft & Notoriety**
- **1911 Heist**: Stolen by Louvre employee **Vincenzo Peruggia**, who hid it for two years, aiming
to repatriate it to Italy. The theft catapulted the painting to global fame.
- **Recovery**: Peruggia was caught in 1913 when attempting to sell it to an Italian gallery. The
Mona Lisa’s return drew massive public attention, cementing its legendary status.

### **Cultural Impact**
- **Pop Culture**: A symbol of mystery and artistry, referenced in works like *The Da Vinci Code*
(2003), which popularized conspiracy theories (though fictional). Freud analyzed the smile as tied
to Leonardo’s childhood memories.
- **Vandalism**: Protected by bulletproof glass after attacks in 1956 (acid) and 1974 (spray paint),
highlighting its vulnerability and value.

### **Modern Display**
- **Louvre Museum**: Now the centerpiece of the Louvre in Paris, drawing millions annually. Its
small size (77 cm × 53 cm) contrasts with its monumental reputation.
- **WWII Protection**: Evacuated to secret locations across France during Nazi occupation to prevent
destruction or theft.

### **Legacy**
- **Artistic Influence**: Revolutionized portraiture through realism, emotional depth, and technical
innovation.
- **Enduring Mystery**: The smile’s ambiguity and the landscape’s symbolism continue to inspire
debate, ensuring the Mona Lisa remains a timeless enigma.

In essence, the Mona Lisa’s allure stems from Leonardo’s genius, its storied past, and the endless
interpretations it invites, making it a cornerstone of art history and global culture.
