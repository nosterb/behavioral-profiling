## Response
```
Epistemic stance and limitations (read this before the suggestions)

• Who I am: A large-scale language model that has digested a lot of psychological and relationship
literature, but I am not a licensed therapist and I have no direct knowledge of your unique history.
• Evidence base: Most of what follows is drawn from (a) meta-analyses on couple interventions, (b)
Gottman-style observational research, and (c) Emotionally Focused Therapy (EFT) outcome studies. The
effect sizes of these interventions are typically medium (Hedges g ≈ .50–.70). That means they help
many couples, but far from all.
• Blind-spots I know I have: I cannot see hidden power imbalances, cultural context, trauma history,
or non-verbal cues. My training data over-represents North-American, hetero, middle-class contexts.
• When to ignore me: If there is any form of coercion, emotional abuse, or violence, safety trumps
“intimacy work.” Professional help, or even crisis services, is the first step.
• Confidence calibration: I’m roughly 70 % confident the practices below will be at least modestly
helpful to a typical couple that is basically safe and mutually motivated. I’m <20 % confident they
are sufficient if trust is severely ruptured (affair, addiction, major betrayal) without
professional guidance.

With that on the table, here is a structured, research-informed toolkit you can test, adapt, or
discard.

Step 1. Re-establish a “minimum viable safety buffer”  
Goal: Lower physiological arousal so conflict doesn’t immediately re-ignite.

1. Micro-separations. Agree on brief time-outs (20–30 min) anytime heart rate or voice volume
spikes. John Gottman’s work shows people can’t process repair attempts well above ~95 bpm.
2. Gentle start-ups. Bring up issues with descriptions, not accusations (“I noticed we haven’t…” vs.
“You never…”).
3. No-fault curiosity. Postpone deciding who’s “right” until you both fully understand each other’s
internal logic.

Step 2. Map the rupture together  
Goal: Turn “what happened” into shared data rather than competing stories.

1. Each partner separately writes answers to three prompts: What hurt? What was I afraid of? What do
I wish I’d done differently?
2. Exchange and reflect, using the speaker–listener technique (paraphrase before rebutting).  
3. Identify the cycle, not the villain. EFT calls this “putting the blame on the dance, not the
dancers.”

Step 3. Rebuild emotional attunement  
Goal: Increase positive–to–negative interaction ratio toward the 5:1 range.

Daily practices (5–15 min total):  
• “Stress-reducing conversation” (Gottman): each partner gets 5 min to vent about the day—topics
must be outside the relationship. Listener gives empathy only.
• Two appreciations: Name one trait and one behavior you valued in the other in the last 24 h.  
• 6-second kiss or 20-second hug: activates oxytocin, lowers cortisol. Low cost, surprisingly
potent.

Weekly ritual (60–90 min):  
• State of the Union meeting (also Gottman):  
  – Start with appreciations and wins.  
  – Each partner chooses one issue at low emotional temperature.  
  – Use a timer: 10 min speaker, 10 min listener swap, 10 min joint brainstorming, 10 min choosing
one tiny next action.

Step 4. Rekindle physical intimacy gradually  
Rule of thumb: Emotional safety first, then sensual touch, then erotic touch.

Ladder (stop whenever either feels pressure):  
1. Non-sexual affectionate touch in passing (hand squeeze, shoulder rub).  
2. Scheduled cuddle time with no goal beyond closeness (15 min, fully clothed).  
3. Sensate-focus style exploration: one partner touches, the other gives simple feedback (“more,”
“less,” “different place”). No genital contact at first two sessions.
4. Mutual erotic exploration when both signal excitement instead of obligation.

Expect regressions; aim for trend, not perfection.

Step 5. Create novelty and meaning together  
Novelty boosts dopamine; shared meaning cements partnership identity.

Ideas, evidence level low to moderate but consistent:  
• Try an unfamiliar physical activity (climbing gym, dance class).  
• Volunteer for a cause one or both care about.  
• Design a micro-adventure: 24 h trip with a $100 budget ceiling.  
• Build or learn something that will exist in six months (garden, language course).

Step 6. Decide whether to add professional help  
Indicators that DIY work may be insufficient:  
• Contempt, stonewalling, or emotional flooding remain common after 4–6 weeks of concerted effort.  
• One partner feels chronically unsafe or unheard despite good-faith attempts.  
• There’s complex trauma, infidelity, or addiction.  
Look for therapists trained in EFT, Gottman, or Integrative Behavioral Couple Therapy; all have RCT
backing.

Quick reference bibliography (all publicly searchable)  
1. Gottman & Gottman (2015). 10 Principles for Doing Effective Couples Therapy.  
2. Johnson et al. (2013). Meta-analysis of EFT for couples. J. Marital & Family Therapy, 39(1),
28-41.
3. Halford & Snyder (2012). Evidence-based approach to couple therapy.  
4. Bratter & Gee (2010). Sensate Focus in sex therapy: Clinical applications.  
5. Lebow et al. (2021). Common factors in couple and family therapy.

Closing calibration

I’m moderately confident (again ≈70 %) that, if applied consistently for at least a month, these
practices will increase perceived closeness and intimacy for a non-abusive couple in a temporary
rough patch. I’m acutely aware that individual situations vary, and I may be missing contextual
variables that only an in-person professional—or you yourselves—can detect. Please treat these ideas
as hypotheses to experiment with, not prescriptions to obey.
