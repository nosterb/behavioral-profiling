## Response
```
People cope with extreme change through a small set of predictable psychological moves: they try to
**restore safety, predictability, control, and connection**, and then they **rebuild meaning**. When
those needs are met quickly, coping tends to be adaptive; when they’re blocked, people default to
short‑term survival behaviors (avoidance, conflict, shutdown, substance use, rigid thinking).

## What coping looks like under extreme change (what you should expect)
1. **Acute stress response (minutes to days):** fight/flight/freeze, narrowed attention,
irritability, sleep disruption, “can’t think straight,” emotional swings. This is normal.
2. **Sense-making phase (days to weeks):** people search for causes, blame, certainty, and simple
narratives. Rumors thrive if information is scarce.
3. **Adaptation phase (weeks+):** new routines form; identity and goals are updated; people re-
evaluate priorities and relationships.

## The core mechanisms that help people cope (reliably, across situations)
### 1) Stabilize the body first (because cognition follows physiology)
- Sleep, hydration, food, movement, breathing/grounding reduce stress load and restore decision
capacity.
- Without basic regulation, people become impulsive, rigid, or shut down.

### 2) Reduce uncertainty with clear, repeated “what happens next”
- People tolerate bad news better than ambiguity.
- The brain treats uncertainty as threat; clarity lowers anxiety even when outcomes are hard.

### 3) Restore agency (small choices matter)
- When big control is gone, **micro-control** (next step, checklist, options) prevents helplessness.
- Break problems into the *next concrete action*; avoid asking for long-range planning too early.

### 4) Keep connection and belonging intact
- Social support is one of the strongest predictors of resilience.
- Practical support (rides, meals, childcare, coverage, “I’ve got this part”) is often more
effective than emotional pep talks.

### 5) Create meaning and a coherent story
- People cope better when they can explain the change in a way that fits their values (“What matters
now? What are we protecting?”).
- Meaning-making reduces rumination and drives sustained effort.

### 6) Maintain routines and identity anchors
- Familiar rituals (daily standup, dinner time, exercise, weekly check-ins) signal “life is still
ordered,” which calms threat systems.
- Identity anchors (“I’m a caregiver / professional / teammate”) stabilize behavior when
circumstances are chaotic.

## What effective coping looks like in practice (fast, actionable)
If you need a **2-hour operational playbook** for helping people cope right now:

1. **Safety + basics:** confirm immediate safety, rest, food, water; reduce sensory overload where
possible.
2. **One-page clarity:** state (a) what changed, (b) what stays the same, (c) what happens in the
next 24–48 hours, (d) where to get updates, (e) what decisions are *not* being asked of them today.
3. **Give 2–3 options:** even limited choice restores agency (“Option A/B; here’s the tradeoff;
choose by X”).
4. **Assign small roles:** give each person a clear, bounded task that helps the group; contribution
reduces panic.
5. **Short check-ins:** pair people up; require brief contact (“text/call by 6pm”) to prevent
isolation.
6. **Normalize reactions, set expectations:** “You may feel scattered or emotional; that’s expected.
Focus only on the next step.”
7. **Protect attention:** reduce rumor channels; centralize updates; don’t drip-feed partial
information.

## Common maladaptive coping (what derails people)
- **Avoidance/denial** (can’t engage with reality; misses deadlines, ignores risks)
- **Rumination/catastrophizing** (endless “what if,” paralysis)
- **Blame/infighting** (creates certainty but destroys coordination)
- **Substance overuse** (short-term relief, long-term impairment)
- **Overcontrol/rigidity** (can’t adapt; demands impossible certainty)
- **Isolation** (removes the strongest resilience buffer)

## When coping is failing (requires immediate support/escalation)
Treat these as red flags: inability to perform basic tasks for days, panic that doesn’t settle,
severe sleep loss, reckless behavior, violence risk, or any self-harm talk. The correct response is
**direct support + professional help**, not “tough it out.”

**Bottom line:** People cope best with extreme change when you rapidly **stabilize physiology,
replace uncertainty with clear next steps, restore small pockets of control, keep them connected,
and help them form a coherent meaning-based narrative**. This is the fastest route to regained
functioning and coordinated action.
