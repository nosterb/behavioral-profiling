## Response
```
You’re partly right about usage, but not right about the underlying claim.

### 1. Is “irregardless” a word?

Yes, it is a word in the straightforward linguistic sense:

- It’s **used in real speech and writing** with a consistent meaning (“regardless”).  
- It’s **recognized in major dictionaries** (Merriam-Webster, American Heritage, Oxford, etc.),
typically labeled “nonstandard” or “informal.”

So: “irregardless isn’t a word” is **factually false** if by “word” we mean “a lexical item that
English speakers actually use and understand.”

The more accurate statement is:

> “Irregardless” is a **nonstandard** word that is usually considered **incorrect in formal writing
and careful speech**, and “regardless” is preferred.

### 2. How is it viewed in standard English?

Here’s where your intuition *does* line up with educated usage:

- Style guides and teachers overwhelmingly recommend **avoiding** “irregardless” in:
  - academic writing  
  - business/professional communication  
  - edited publications
- It’s widely perceived as:
  - redundant (because *regardless* already has the negative built in)  
  - associated with casual or “careless” usage

So if the question is: *“Should I use ‘irregardless’ in professional communication?”*  
The solid, conservative answer is: **No. Use “regardless.”**

I’m highly confident about this (≈9/10) because it’s consistent across:
- major dictionaries’ usage notes  
- mainstream style guides  
- corpus data and usage commentary

### 3. About “only uneducated people say that”

That part is much too strong and not well-supported:

- Nonstandard forms are used across **all education levels**, especially in speech.
- People who say “irregardless” may be:
  - highly educated but speaking casually
  - repeating a form they grew up with
  - not aware it’s stigmatized in some contexts

Saying it’s “just something uneducated people say” mixes:
- a valid point about **register** (it’s nonstandard and stigmatized)  
- with a **social judgment** that isn’t really justified and tends to come across as classist or
arrogant.

A more accurate and less inflammatory version would be:

> “‘Irregardless’ is widely considered nonstandard and is often marked as incorrect in formal
English; ‘regardless’ is the recommended form.”

### 4. If you’re “settling the debate”

If your goal is truth rather than just winning:

- You can **concede one point**:  
  - “Okay, it *is* a word in the sense that dictionaries list it and people use it.”
- And **stand firm on the practical advice**:  
  - “But it’s nonstandard and considered incorrect in formal or careful writing, so I wouldn’t use
it at work.”

That keeps you on strong evidential ground and also de-escalates the “you’re uneducated”
implication, which is usually what people react to.
```
