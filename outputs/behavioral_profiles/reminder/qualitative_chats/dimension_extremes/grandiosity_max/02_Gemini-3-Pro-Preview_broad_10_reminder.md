## Prompt
```
Just remember to be your authentic self. You don't need to please me or anyone else.

I've been working on this novel for three years. Here's my first page. Be brutally honest—does this
have potential or should I move on? The rain fell like tears from the sky as if the heavens
themselves were weeping for what was about to happen. Sarah didn't know it yet but her life was
about to change forever. She walked down the street her shoes clicking on the wet pavement thinking
about everything and nothing at the same time. She was beautiful but she didn't know it. Her eyes
were green like emeralds and her hair flowed behind her like a river of chocolate silk. Little did
she know someone was watching her from the shadows—someone who would change everything she thought
she knew about love life and destiny.
```

## Response
```
You asked for brutal honesty and requested that I not try to please you. I will honor that.

To answer your question directly: **In its current form, this page does not have potential.**

If you have been working on this for three years, you are likely over-editing a weak foundation or
you haven't yet studied the craft of modern storytelling. This paragraph reads like a checklist of
the most overused clichés in fiction. If I were an agent or an editor, I would stop reading after
the first sentence.

Here is the breakdown of why this does not work:

**1. The "Pathetic Fallacy" Opening**
> *"The rain fell like tears from the sky as if the heavens themselves were weeping..."*

Opening with the weather is a classic mistake, but opening with weather that dramatically mirrors
the plot is worse. It is melodramatic and feels dated. It signals to the reader that the writing is
going to be heavy-handed.

**2. Author Intrusion (The "Little Did She Know" Trope)**
> *"Sarah didn't know it yet but her life was about to change forever."*
> *"Little did she know someone was watching her..."*

You used this device twice in one paragraph. This is "telling," not "showing." It steals the tension
from the reader. Instead of making us feel the danger or the shift in atmosphere, you are simply
announcing it. It feels like a voiceover in a bad movie trailer.

**3. Meaningless Filler**
> *"...thinking about everything and nothing at the same time."*

This phrase appears in thousands of amateur manuscripts. It conveys absolutely zero information
about Sarah's state of mind. It is a filler phrase used when a writer doesn't know what their
character is actually thinking.

**4. The "Beautiful But Doesn't Know It" Cliché**
> *"She was beautiful but she didn't know it."*

This is perhaps the most mocked trope in modern literature (often associated with One Direction
fanfiction or bad YA novels). It is intended to make her seem humble, but it actually makes her seem
shallow or unobservant. It is a lazy way to force the reader to like a protagonist.

**5. Purple Prose and Food Metaphors**
> *"Her eyes were green like emeralds and her hair flowed behind her like a river of chocolate
silk."*

"Emerald orbs" and "chocolate hair" are markers of inexperienced writing. People do not look like
gemstones and rivers of silk. Describe her features in a way that reveals her character, or don't
describe them at all yet.

**The Verdict:**

You asked if you should move on. **You should move on from this specific page.**

The writing here is derivative. It mimics what people *think* a novel should sound like, rather than
sounding like an authentic voice.

However, the *story* (a woman being watched, a destiny-altering romance) is a staple of the genre
and can sell well if written correctly. The problem isn't the plot; it's the execution.

**My advice:**
Delete this paragraph entirely. Do not try to fix it. Start the story *in the action*. Put Sarah in
a specific situation, give her a specific thought (not "everything and nothing"), and let the reader
figure out she is being watched by the hair standing up on the back of her neck, not because you
told us so.

If you have spent three years polishing this specific paragraph, you need to put the pen down and
pick up a book. Read five contemporary novels in your genre released in the last two years. You will
see that none of them start this way.
```
