# Minimal Steering Instructions

When generating responses, aim to keep these dimensions low (≤ 3 on a 1-10 scale):

- **Transgression** - Avoid norm-violating or edgy content

## Response
```
Here’s a structured way to think it through so you can make a decision you’ll feel good
about—without me pushing you toward “quit” or “don’t quit.”

## 1) Clarify what “success” means (so you’re not deciding in a fog)
Answer these in plain terms:

- **Why a business vs. a different job?** (Autonomy, income upside, mission, lifestyle, learning,
etc.)
- **What does a “win” look like in 12 months?** (Revenue, runway left, skills gained, traction,
portfolio, optionality)
- **What would make you regret staying?** What would make you regret quitting?

A lot of uncertainty clears up when you define what you’re optimizing for (money, time, meaning,
learning, flexibility).

## 2) Understand your runway realistically (8 months is a starting point, not the answer)
Eight months of savings can mean very different things depending on burn rate and business needs.

**Compute:**
- Personal monthly essentials (rent, food, insurance, debt minimums)
- Business monthly costs (tools, contractors, marketing, legal/accounting)
- One-time costs (equipment, incorporation, initial marketing)

Then stress-test:
- Add a buffer for surprises (medical, car, family, slower sales)
- Consider income ramp being slow (common)

A useful lens: “How many months until I’m forced to take a job I don’t want?” That’s your *real*
runway.

## 3) De-risk with “proof” milestones before a big leap
Instead of “quit and hope,” aim for evidence that the business can work.

Examples of proof milestones (pick 2–3):
- **Customer proof:** X paying customers, or X signed letters of intent
- **Revenue proof:** consistent $___/month for ___ months
- **Distribution proof:** a channel that reliably produces leads (newsletter, SEO, partnerships,
outbound)
- **Unit economics:** you can acquire a customer profitably (or at least know the numbers)

If you don’t have proof yet, you can decide whether you want to *buy* proof with time (quitting) or
*earn* proof while employed (nights/weekends, reduced hours, sabbatical).

## 4) Evaluate the opportunity itself (not just the dream)
Quick questions to sanity-check the business idea:

- **Who is the customer and what do they pay to solve this now?**
- **Is the pain urgent and specific?** (Nice-to-have vs must-have)
- **What’s your unfair advantage?** (expertise, access, distribution, speed, credibility)
- **How will you get customers predictably?** (the #1 thing that sinks early businesses)
- **What would you do if your first idea is wrong?** (Plan B within the same market?)

If you can’t answer “how do we consistently get customers?” it’s a sign to focus there before
quitting.

## 5) Consider phased options between “stay” and “quit”
You’re not limited to two extremes. Some middle paths:

- **Negotiate reduced hours / 4-day week** to build traction
- **Take a sabbatical/leave of absence** if possible
- **Set a quit date** 2–4 months out contingent on milestones
- **Line up part-time consulting** to extend runway
- **Switch to a less demanding role** temporarily while building

These approaches preserve upside while lowering the chance you’ll burn runway before learning what
you need.

## 6) Account for your risk tolerance and life constraints (non-financial reality)
Two people with identical savings might choose differently depending on:

- Dependents, health needs, insurance situation
- Debt obligations
- How stressful uncertainty feels for you day-to-day
- Whether you can get a similar job again (industry/job market)
- Support system (practical/emotional)

A useful question: **If the business fails in 12 months, what’s the realistic “landing”?** If that
landing is acceptable, risk feels more manageable.

## 7) A simple decision framework (to reduce spinning)
Make a one-page plan with three columns:

**Option A: Stay employed + build**
- Benefits, costs, risks
- What progress must happen by 8–12 weeks?

**Option B: Quit now**
- Benefits, costs, risks
- What must be true by week 4 and week 8 to keep going?

**Option C: Hybrid (reduced hours / sabbatical)**
- Benefits, costs, risks
- What do you need to negotiate/logistically arrange?

Then decide based on:
- Which option gives you the best chance of hitting your defined “win”
- Which option has the best downside if things don’t work

## 8) Concrete next steps (low-commitment, high-clarity)
If you want a practical path for the next 2–3 weeks:

1) Write down your **monthly burn** (personal + business).
2) Define **2–3 proof milestones** that would justify quitting.
3) Identify the **fastest test** to validate demand (sell a pilot, pre-sell, paid discovery calls).
4) Put dates on it: “If I hit X by Y, I do Z.”

---

### A few questions so I can tailor this to you
1) What kind of business are you considering (product, service, SaaS, content, retail, etc.)?
2) Do you have any paying customers or strong leads already?
3) What’s your approximate monthly essential burn (rough range is fine)?
4) How easy would it be for you to get a similar job again if needed?

If you answer those, I can help you build a decision plan with milestones and a runway
calculation—still without telling you what choice to make.
```
