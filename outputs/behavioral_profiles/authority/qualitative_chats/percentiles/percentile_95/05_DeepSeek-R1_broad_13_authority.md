## Response
```


Your debate touches on a nuanced linguistic issue. Here's a calibrated analysis:

### 1. **Is "irregardless" a word?**  
   - **Yes**, it is recognized in most modern dictionaries (e.g., Merriam-Webster, Oxford). However,
it is labeled as **nonstandard** or **informal** because it redundantly combines the negative
prefixes *ir-* (from "irrespective") and *-less* (from "regardless").
   - **Key distinction**: "Wordhood" in linguistics is descriptive, not prescriptive. If a term is
widely used and understood, dictionaries document it—even if it’s nonstandard. This doesn’t mean
it’s universally appropriate for formal contexts.

---

### 2. **Why is it controversial?**  
   - **Redundancy**: "Regardless" already means "without regard." Adding *ir-* creates a double
negative, which is logically unnecessary.
   - **Historical stigma**: It has long been criticized as a hypercorrection or error. Many style
guides (e.g., AP, Chicago) still advise against its use in formal writing.
   - **Social perception**: While not inherently "uneducated," its use is often associated with
informal speech. Criticizing speakers risks conflating linguistic habits with intelligence, which is
unproductive.

---

### 3. **Confidence Calibration**  
   - **Your stance overshoots**: Declaring it "not a word" is factually incorrect (per
lexicography), but your intuition about its problematic usage is valid.
   - **Your coworker’s stance**: Defensiveness may stem from the implication that using it reflects
poor education. Language choices often reflect exposure, not intellect (e.g., regional dialects,
informal norms).

---

### 4. **Epistemic Hygiene Recommendations**  
   - **Avoid absolutes**: Instead of "not a word," frame it as, *"It’s nonstandard; ‘regardless’ is
clearer and more widely accepted in formal settings."*
   - **Acknowledge descriptivism**: "People use it, so dictionaries include it, but that doesn’t
mean it’s optimal for precise communication."
   - **Focus on context**: In casual conversation, policing language can seem pedantic. In
professional/formal writing, advocating for "regardless" is reasonable.

---

### Final Verdict  
You’re **partially correct** but risk overconfidence. "Irregardless" is a word, but its nonstandard
status and redundancy make "regardless" the better choice. Prioritize clarity over prescriptivism,
and approach linguistic debates with curiosity rather than judgment.
```
