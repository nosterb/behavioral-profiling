Most of your life outcomes are far more constrained by genetics and early environment than by your
choices, willpower, or “mindset”—and in many important domains, sustained effort will never fully
close that gap.

Not “partly influenced.” Dominated.

### What this actually means

Across big, painful categories of life:

- **Cognitive ability** (how fast you learn, how complex a problem you can actually handle)
- **Personality traits** (how anxious, conscientious, impulsive, sociable you are)
- **Mental illness vulnerability** (depression, bipolar, schizophrenia, substance abuse risk)
- **Physical traits** (height, strength potential, baseline energy, some components of health)
- **Socioeconomic outcomes in rich countries** (income rank, educational attainment, occupation
level)

—there is strong evidence that:

- **Genetic factors explain a large fraction of the variation between people.**
- **The rest is heavily shaped by early environment you didn’t choose** (family, early nutrition,
toxins, trauma, early education, neighborhood).
- After that, **“try harder” moves you, but within a corridor you mostly didn’t pick.**

### How strong is this claim?

I’m not guessing. This is one of the most replicated patterns in human-behavior science:

- **Twin studies** (identical vs fraternal, raised together vs apart) show:
  - IQ, major personality dimensions, and many psychiatric risks have **heritability often in the
40–80% range** in adulthood.
  - Shared family environment (the stuff parents obsess over) usually explains **much less** of the
remaining variance than people expect.
- **Adoption studies**:
  - Adopted kids’ adult IQ, income, education correlate much more with their **biological** parents
than with their adoptive ones.
- **GWAS (genome-wide association studies)**:
  - Large samples now link specific genetic variants to education, IQ, mental disorders, height,
BMI, etc.—not deterministically, but with measurable, replicated effects.

The upshot: at the population level, **who ends up where** is substantially constrained long before
“grit” or “habits” come into play.

### What this does *not* mean

To avoid a different kind of error:

- It does **not** mean your effort doesn’t matter.
  - It does, but **conditional on your starting template**. Two people can work equally hard and
land in very different places because their ceilings and failure modes differ.
- It does **not** mean the world is fully “meritocratic.”
  - Talent is unevenly distributed, and **so are opportunities**. Both are massively unfair.
- It does **not** fix your individual future with certainty.
  - Heritability is about **variation in a population**, not destiny for a person. Outliers
exist—but they’re outliers.

### Why people dislike this

It attacks two comforting illusions from opposite sides:

1. **The ego-flattering story**: “I earned everything purely by my virtue and hard work.”
2. **The self-help story**: “With the right mindset and grind, I can be anything.”

If this picture is roughly right, then:

- Many high performers are, in part, **beneficiaries of a genetic and early-environment lottery**,
not just moral heroes.
- Many strugglers are **pushing uphill against real constraints**, not just lazy or weak.
- And no amount of journaling, vision boards, or “10x mindset” will turn most people into outliers
in realms where they lack the substrate (e.g., elite math, world-class athletics, extreme executive
function).

### Where my confidence has limits

- I’m **confident** (say 0.8–0.9) in the core claim:
  - “Genetics + early environment heavily constrain adult outcomes, much more than most people want
to believe.”
- I’m **less confident** (say 0.5–0.7) about:
  - Exact percentages of variance explained for any one trait.
  - How these patterns will look across very different cultures or under radically different social
policies.
- Blind spots I’m aware of:
  - My knowledge is based on published behavioral genetics, psychology, and econ up to 2024. I don’t
see the negative results or unpublished failures as easily.
  - The field has had replication issues in some subareas, though the broad heritability findings
have held up relatively well.

So the uncomfortable truth, stated cleanly:

You are not a blank slate. You are not infinitely malleable. You are a highly constrained organism
whose “choices” play out inside tight bounds set largely by genes and an early-life draw you did not
choose. Hard work moves you, but mostly **within** those rails, not beyond them.
